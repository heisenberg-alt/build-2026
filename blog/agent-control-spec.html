ACS is the most important Build announcement nobody noticed

Frontier models get the headlines. Open contracts get the install base. The Agent Control Specification is the part of Build that lines up most directly with Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard — and the one I think history will judge most kindly.

By Heisenberg

Tucked between Frontier Tuning and the Agent Optimization Loop on Day 2, Microsoft published the Agent Control Specification (ACS) — a vendor-neutral spec for runtime agent governance. For me, this is the announcement that lines up most directly with Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard, and the one I think history will judge most kindly. It's also the strongest recent signal that Microsoft sees agent trust as an ecosystem problem, not a single-vendor one.

What Microsoft announced

ACS is a published, open specification with a reference implementation in both Foundry and the Copilot app on day one. It covers the four messy middles of every agent deployment so far: what the agent is allowed to do, who needs to approve sensitive actions, what data it's allowed to touch, and how every step is audited.

  • Declarative manifest of the agent's capabilities and tools
  • Per-action approval hooks: silent, prompt, or multi-party
  • Data-residency and PII boundaries expressed inside the contract
  • Runtime-agnostic audit log format — portable across vendors
  • Built on existing identity and policy primitives — no rip-and-replace

Why it lines up with Microsoft's direction

Microsoft's six Responsible AI principles — fairness, reliability and safety, privacy and security, inclusiveness, transparency, and accountability — only mean something if engineers have a place to express them in code. ACS is that place for agents. It takes principles that have lived in policy documents and turns them into a manifest that runtimes can enforce uniformly.

It also continues a pattern Microsoft has been quietly executing for years: open-source the contract, ship the best reference, and let the ecosystem build on top. The Language Server Protocol did it for editors. OpenAPI did it for APIs. MCP did it for tools. ACS is the same playbook for agent governance — which is exactly the surface where a single vendor's policy file would be the worst possible outcome.

My take

The part that made me sit up: ACS isn't trying to be Microsoft's policy layer with extra YAML. It's trying to be the contract that makes governance portable across Foundry, Copilot, Windows, and whatever third-party runtime you adopt next. That's a much more generous posture than the market expected, and it's the right one if you actually believe agents will outlive any single vendor's runtime.

For Microsoft, this is also a credibility play — and a welcome one. The last few years have made it clear that the company wants to be the trusted home for enterprise AI, and trust isn't a marketing surface, it's an engineering one. ACS is the strongest signal yet that Microsoft understands the difference, and is willing to invest in the open contracts that earn that trust over time.

— Heisenberg, 4 June 2026 · specs rarely make highlight reels, but they're often what's left standing five years later.
